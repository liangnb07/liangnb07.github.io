# The Art of Spending Money 
## Morgan Housel
#### 2026-06-01

Isn't it strange I don't want a huge house, or a fancy car, or high end vacations? Housel claimed that for many people, these items were what they desired and which people deemed were worthy of garnering them more respect and admiration from peers -- i.e, they believed that these material goods were valuable enough that others valued them. 

But I don't want a huge house or any of those specific material goods Housel describes as "the classic trap". I also don't align myself with being the type to live a simple, quiet life. Paradoxial? No, I do desire money, but I just don't obsess over flaunting myself and impressing others.

I don't want a huge house but rather I prefer to live in a studio apartment in a huge, bustling, alive city - I don't want a car but rather a walkable city, public transit, and subway systems. Am I cheap, or frugal? I don't know. I wouldn't say so - I do have many things I would like to spend money on, given I have money I don't enjoy vacations unless it's somewhere providing of actual interest to me (e.g, the e-earphone in Akihabara, Tokyo), otherwise it forces me to pretend like I enjoy being on a beach (I don't) and doesn't let me put that money into what I actually want.

It is my belief that when Housel writes about a huge house, a luxury car, or a high-end vacation being the "endgame," he is describing a societal default, not a universal human truth. For most, spending money is less about the item itself and more about buying a temporary feeling: status, respect, and validation from peers.

The irony, which Housel often points out, is the Man in the Car Paradox:

> "You might see someone driving a $200,000 Ferrari and think, "Wow, if I had that car, people would think I’m cool." But in reality, you aren't looking at the driver; you're imagining yourself in the driver's seat. No one is actually respecting the person holding the asset—they are just projecting their own desires onto it."

I consider myself to have many hobbies. And so, when I was reading this book, I constantly thought that the situations Housel described were understandable, yet absurd.
